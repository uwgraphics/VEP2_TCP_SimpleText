A direction to English Scholars, showing them by a plain and easy way, bow to
pronounce and read Welsh perfectly.
THE Welsh and English Alphabets are but the same characters, and some of them
bears the same sound or pronunciation in the Welsh, as they do in the English;
and those that bears the same sound in both Languages are these; a b d ff h l m
n o p ph r s: All the other letters that are in the Welsh Alphabet, are these;
c ch dd e f g i ll ng t th u w y: And these do differ in their sound or
pronunciation from the English way, viz.

C is always pronounced in the Welsh, as k is in the English, and bears the
same sound as k all along; and is never pronounced in the Welsh, as it is in
these following English words; ceasing, celestial, precious, precepts, prince,
deceit, deceive, price, proceed, mercy, etc.

Ch is counted as one letter in the Welsh, and there is not any letter that
sounds in the English, as ch does in the Welsh; it is ch that makes the
Welch-men to wrattle in the Throat* as the Englishmen calls it. The best way
that I can direct you to understand the true pronunciation of ch in the Welsh
tongue, is, to set down first the English words of the Welsh that has ch in
them, and then second those English words, with the true translated Welsh words
for the same; and by that help, he that can read English, and understand the
speaking of Welsh, cannot choose but quickly learn the true pronunciation of ch
in the Welsh tongue. English for the opposite Welsh. Cymraeg i'r saesnag ar
gyfer. Six, complaining, high. Chwech, achwyn, uchel. Looking, a looking-glass.
Edrych, drch. A hill side, a market. Llechwedd, marchnad. Overcome, a small
quantity. Trechu, ychydig.

Observe, That ch is never pronounced in the Welsh, as in these English words,
viz. chance, chide, chosen, Scholar, School, Schism, etc.

DD is used as one letter in the Welsh, and is never pronounced in the Welsh as
in the English: dd sounds in the Welsh tongue always as th sounds in these
following English words; father, brother, either, rather, thou, etc.

E (when thus, e) without a circumflex, sounds in the Welsh after its own
proper sound as in these following English words; bend, bell, belt, etc.

E (when thus, e) with a circumflex, carries in the Welsh the complete sound of
ea, as in these following English words; bears, wears, weak, etc. Note: That e
never sounds in the Welsh, as it does in these English words; me, he, she, we,
three, be, free, etc. Also, that e never loses its sound in the Welsh as it
does in these English words; where, dare, spare, care, one, store, are, more,
etc.

F is never pronounced in the Welsh as in the English; it bears the same sound
in the Welsh tongue as v does in these following English words; have, brave,
grave, vile, violent, villain, etc.

G is never pronounced but one way in the Welsh, and that is, as it sounds in
these following English words; against, agony, ague, nag, wag, bag, etc. Note,
that g never sounds, nor is pronounced in the Welsh, as in the following
English words; age, george, gentleman, eight, light, weight, angel, danger,
obliged, judge, judging, etc.

I is not always pronounced in the Welsh as in the English, but for the most
part carries the same sound in the Welsh as it does in these following English
words; nothing, beginning, learning, something, etc. but when thus, i, with a
circumflex over the head of it, it sounds then in the Welsh tongue as ee sounds
in these following English words; weed, breed, need, teeth, etc. Note, That i
never sounds, nor is pronounced in the Welsh tongue, as in these English words;
pain, grain, pipe, bright, sight, fire, file, james, jealous, jews, jones,
joyful, etc.

Ll. There is not any letter that sounds, or can be pronounced in the English
tongue, as ll is in the Welsh; therefore the best and readiest way that I can
direct you to understand the true pronunciation of ll in the Welsh tongue, is,
To set you down first some English words of those Welsh words that has ll in
them, and then second those English words, with the true translated Welsh words
for the same; and by that means, he that can read English, and understand the
speaking of the Welsh, may quickly learn the true pronunceation of ll in the
Welsh tongue.

English for the opposite Welsh. Cymraeg i'r saesnaeg gyferbyn. Sharp, hair,
bare, dust. Llym, gwallt, llwm, llwch. Milk, lion, quietness, a hand. Llaeth,
llew, llonydd, llaw. Filling, sportful, up hill. Llenwi, llon, allt. Knife,
razor, understand. Cyllell, ellym, deallt.

Note also, That ll is never pronounced in Welsh, as it is (...) these
following English words; all, allow, Allelujah, bells, ^ellow, belly, hill,
fellow, follow, folly, etc.

Ng. When ng are thus placed in the Welsh, they are counted as one letter, and
do always keep the same sound as they bear in these following English words;
spring, bring, longing, England, etc.

Note also, That g in the Welsh never forsakes n, to cleave to another
syllable, as in these following English words; angel, danger, engaged,
engraved, etc.

T carries its own proper sound always in the Welsh, as it does in these
following English words; prate, not, at, that, tongue, teach, ten, times, etc.
T is never pronounced in the Welsh, as in the following words; condition,
congregation, consecration, consolation, perfection, etc.

Th sounds always in the Welsh as in these following words; theft, thieves,
threaten, throat, thrust, through, south, mouth, teeth. Th never sounds in the
Welsh, as in father, mother* brother, other, etc.

U never changes its sound in the Welsh tongue, but always keeps to the same
sound in Welsh, as i bears in these following English words; brain, gain, pain,
etc. U never bears in the Welsh any of these sounds that it bears in the
following words; even, evil, void, visit, delude, refuse, choose, upright,
uncle, utterance, etc.

W, when without a circumflex* sounds always in the Welsh as in these following
English words; twice, twenty, wander, went, way, etc. but when thus, w, with a
circumflex over, it sounds then in Welsh as oo sounds in these following
English words; loose, soon, roon, moon, etc.

Y is never pronounced in the Welsh as in the English, and yet it sounds two
several ways in the Welsh tongue, viz. when y is without a circumflex, it
sounds in the Welsh as u sounds in these following words; dull, dust, sun,
begun, etc. but when thus,, with a circumflex over it, it agrees with the sound
of u in the Welsh, and that is, as i sounds in the following English words;
brain, grain, gain, pain, etc.

K q x z are not Welsh letters, and therefore they are never used in the Welsh,
but in proper names.

For your better understanding of the Welsh Vowels, with the circumflex over
them, see the ninth page of this Book.

Were it not for ch and ll, these foregoing Instructions might serve to teach
an English man, that never understood one word of Welsh in his life* the true
pronunciation and perfect reading of the Welsh tongue; but because ch and ll
bears such sound in the Welsh as no letters whatsoever bears in the English, it
is therefore too hard for English people hereby to learn the true reading of
Welsh: But those Welsh people that can read English, and understand the
speaking of Welsh, may with ease hereby attain to the perfect reading of the
Welsh, if they think it not an undervaluing to them to read their
Mother Tongue, as I fear that too many of them do in this age.

I have in the beginning of this Book, by several Authors, and sufficient
Testimonies, proved the Welsh to be the first language in the World, and the
language that God himself spoke to Adam and others for about two thousand years
after the creation; and that first language would have sufficed all the earth
unto the Worlds end, had not the sins of the people provoked God to send
confusion amongst them, as you may find in Genesis 11. to Verse 8.

Can a man own God, and yet be ashamed of that language which God himself chose
first? It would far better become the Welsh men to uphold and extol their own
language, than to cast it away through undervaluing of it. Most people are
prone to embrace evil rather than good; and likewise for the Welsh tongue,
which ought to be looked upon, as it was the only language of the World, and
now there is but few people in the World, but what are ready to scoff and
deride it: But other Tongues and Speeches they much covet, and would gladly
embrace them, although they plainly see, that those are but the fruits of the
people's wickedness.

T.J.
