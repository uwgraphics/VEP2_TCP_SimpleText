To the Reader.
THey that undertake Youth, should facilitate (what they can) the way to
Learning; and all little enough. For every seeming Difficulty (be it ever so
small) is ever and anon driving Boys into despair. That of Composing Theams,
has (as I observe) discomposed many; especially when a bare Thesis from a
Master, without any further conduct, has been given them. Alas! where should
young Beginners meet with either Example or Similitude? That they may be no
more to seek for the one, I have thought of this Expedient, in commending them,
by way of Commonplace, to Gouldmans Dictionary. Dictionaries Boys are never,
or at least, should never be without. Other Authors they many times want.
Therefore as yet I have mentioned no more than what that common Book affords.
And there how easily may Youths be supplied! they will have Examples ready by
them, and upon any occasion. As suppose the Theme of Dulce est pro patria mori,
be given them. Let them turn presently to Codrus, Camillus, Curtius, Decius,
etc. in Gouldmans Poetical Dictionary; which they will find under the Head of
Amor in Patriam in this (...) of mine. I might further explain my self, but my
Book being but short, my Epistle must not be long. Accept of this at present,
and hereafter (if God shall give me life) I will improve it to a far greater
advantage unto Schools.

Your most humble Servant, W. W.

Turpe est in extremo deficere.
A Rude Work and of the first hand has benefitted no Workman; All the labor
is lost, if the last File comes not to it. The Be frames the Wax, that she may
hide up her Honey: Every one builds a House that he may live pleasingly
therein. Not the Work begun, (...) ended, becomes profitable. Who does not
admire the Aeneads of Virgil? but because they were imperfect, by the Author
they were destined to the fire: Who does not think the Venus of Apelles to be
celebrated? but because the last hand was wanting, it was hated by him. Every
Comedy, that it may succeed prosperously, depends upon the last Act; and that
any one may consecrate his Name to Eternity, the last Scene of his Life is to
be expected: In vain has the Integrity of Youth shined forth, when the Impiety
of Old Age betrays it self; It is to be accounted in the place of a Monster;
when as the Poet,

-- Turpiter atrum
Desinit in piscem mulier formosa superne.
Forma Bonum fragile est.
HE that sees a fading Rose, has the flux condition of Beauty set before him;
The North Wind blowing, the Rose withers, and a Disease coming, Beauty
vanishes: Hence it is compared to a Solstitial Herb, which both suddenly rises,
and suddenly falls. That it is good, that does plead, because it consists of
Symmetry, because it draws the eyes of all to it; that it is frail, that does
evince, because it suffers such sudden decays. Helena overcame all in Beauty,
and was accepted of by Paris, but suddenly that comeliness of her Countenance
did languish, and she did complain that her beauty failed. The comeliness of
Beauty how much does it please, but how unstable is it? The Sun, when it is
highest, how soon does it hasten to the fall? And those Graces which sport in
the Face, how suddenly are they brought to their end? Well says the Poet,
Fulgor teneris radians genis Momento rapitur, nallaque non dies Formosi spolium
corporis abstulit. Res est forma fugax.
