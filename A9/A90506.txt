To The Reader.
THis Alphabet (with the manner of the pronouncing and sound of each Letter was
occasioned to be set down here, chiefly for their sakes who who would learn to
read Welsh themselves, or would instruct and teach others to read Welsh: For
many in Wales, Welshmen born, have some knowledge in the English tongue, yet
know nothing in there own language. These may find this Alphabet to be
useful; for they conferring this Welsh Alphabet with their own knowledge and
reading in the English, may come thereby to know the right pronuntion of every
letter and syllable; that so having the true knowledge of the letters in the
Alphabet themselves, they may be better enabled to teach others, their
Household, Friends, and Neighbors, who can read neither English, nor Welsh:
For in many places, people are so ignorant of the Word of God, and so careless
of knowing it, that among twenty Families, there can scarce one Welsh Bible be
found: As for the English Bible, in that Family where any is, it is but
useless, in respect of the generality of those which know nothing, and
understand nothing in that tongue. This carelessness is to be pitied, yet this
ignorance may be remedied, by using or urging this Alphabet upon such as are so
simply ignorant: It will be a means that the Word of God will be better
heeded, however, it will keep men more civil; and in every respect the
knowledge and use of it cannot be amiss. Try it then, and the Lord give his
blessing upon it: This shall be the prayer of every Christian.

If the Learner is ignorant of numbers in Figures, let him observe but the
verses of the Cxix. Psalm, thence he may learn as far as he needs to
turn to any place in the Bible.

The Sound of the Letters.
A. as the English A. B. as the B. in English. C. as the English K. k. Chapter is
pronounced wholly in the throat, the only Welsh letter, like the Greek. chapter
D. as the English D. Dd. has the same sound that th. has in these English
words, viz. this, that, then, though, there, and not so rough as th. in such
words as, viz. think, thief, thought, thrust. E. like the English E. F. like
the v. consonant in English. Ff. like the English F. or Greek ph. G. Like the
letter G, in gate, go, God, good, gold, great, etc. not as the letter G. in
gender, generous, gesture giant. Ng. like Ng. in King, ring, long, etc. H. as
Engl. H. I. like ee in these English words, viz. be, tree, deep, reele, or
like the letter i, in such words as these, rich, ring, bring, thing, sing,
string. L. as the Engl. L. Ll. rougher then the single l. and it has the
pronunciation of lh.i.e. the force of lh. together. M. N. O. P. as they are in
English and Latin. Ph. ph Greek, or like the English f. R. S. T. as they are
in English. Th. th Greek, or th. in this word has. U. like the letter i. in
such words as these, this, is, miss, bliss, kiss, piss, or like, e. in
blew, drew, knew. W. as the oo. in these English words, viz. root, book,
boot, crook, took or as the w. in English. Y. has two sounds; first the
same sound as u. in these English words stir, turn, burn, hunt: or as the
letter i. in these words, third, bird, dirt. Or as the letter i. in these
English words, tin, trim, thin. with this difference, y. in the last
syllable is after the second manner, in every other syllable after the first
manner; examples of both, in these Welsh words, hynny, ystyr, llythyr, bybyr,
myfyr, ymyr, it is also after the first manner in Adverbs of one syllable, or
Prepositions of one syllable, as also in articles, as y. yr. y cyfiawn: yr
Arglywidd in, mynn, ym. But in subjectives or adjectives of Verbs of one
syllable it is after the second manner, as mynn, tyn bryn.

The letter i. in Welsh, having a vowel following it, makes one syllable with
it which follows, as dynion, duwian: unless in such which have a tittle!
(like!) above the letter i. as gweddiau arylywiddiaeth, crio, yspio. W. (in
Welsh) following c. chapter g. ng and a vowell, or n. l. r. with a vowell following
it, loses its sound (as u. in English after a q. as quiet, quart, quarrel)
examples, cweryl, chwech, chwyrn, gwar, gwe gwir, gwohr, gwych, gwlad, gwnaeth,
gwrach. But where w has i above it, there it has its sound, as gwyr crooked,
and gwr men. gwydd presence, and gwd trees, wyn lambs, and wn passion.
