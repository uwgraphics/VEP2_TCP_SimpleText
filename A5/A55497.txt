A NEW BOOK OF MAPPS, BEING A ready Guide or Direction for any Stranger, or
other, who is to Travel in any part of the Commonwealth of England, Scotland,
and Ireland.

By which he may know his way in any part thereof, though he knew it not before.

WHEREIN ARE, I. Alphabeticall Tables, showing the Longitude and Latitude of
all the Towns named in the said Maps; with easy and ready directions how to
find any of them, though you know not in what parts they lie. II. Tables of the
Highways in ENGLAND, WALES, and IRELAND, Alphabetically methodized; which
has made them very plain. III. Tables as easy as an Almanac, which may
supply the use thereof for 100 Years, that is to say, from Anno 1600. to 1700.


And other useful Tables.

By THOMAS PORTER.

This Book being necessary for all men, it is therefore made portable for every
man's pocket.

Licensed, and entered according to the late Act for Printing.

To be sold by ROBERT WALTON, at the Rose and Crown at the West end of S.
Paul's, 1655.

To the Reader.
I Doubt not but it will seem strange to all that know me, that I should
employ the Press upon such a Subject as this, wherein the most familiar of my
acquaintance could never discover in me any such intention. The truth is, I am
no way ambitious of the Title of an Almanackmaker, much less of an Astrologer;
the meanness of the former, and ignominy of the latter, being sufficient to
keep back my desires from running after either. Nevertheless here is
presented to thy view, that which will tell thee the day of the Month, the
fixed Feasts* the mov^able Feasts, the beginning and ending of the Terms* the
hour of Sunset, and indifferently well the age of the Moon for an Hundred
years, the greater part whereof is already expired* This I did, because I my
self have oftener desired to know the time already past, then that which is to
come, and perhaps some others may be of my mind in this particular; and for
such as care not for that, here is near Fifty years to come, which I take to
be a competent time for a thing of this nature: As for the principal Matter
of this Book, although the World abound with plenty of the like, yet have I
never seen any, which to my mean capacity directs so ready a way for the
finding out any Town mentioned therein: and it is not requisite that you should
foreknow what County or part of the Nation the Town is in, which you desire to
find, if you do but know the name thereof; The Maps are small yet will they do
as much as reasonably can be expected in so little room: And if this find a
grateful acceptance, it will encourage me to publish something else of the
like kind. In the mean time I subscribe my self

Thy real Well-wisher, THOMAS PORTER.

WHereas in some places you read maps in the plural number, and in other places
map in the singular number, the word Map is used when both are joined together
as one (as it is best) but when it is bound up in a book, it is too big to be
folded up in one; and therefore each part is folded up by it self, which makes
two maps (the one containing England, Wales, Ireland, and pa r of Scotland; and
the other all the rest of Scotland, together with the form and manner of
London, Westminster, Oxford, and Edinburgh) and in this sense they are called
maps.

By the word (Long Square) you are to understand such a thing as is in the
Margent, marked with A. in the illustration A

midst of it: for the small black lines, crossing the map both ways, makes it
full of such like long squares, so that there is no Town named in the map, but
it must happen to be in some one of those long squares: and every one of them
contains a whole degree, as well in longitude as in latitude: and the beginning
of the book sh^ws you what degree of longitude and latitude any town is in: so
that if you do but know the name only of the Town (though you do not know in
what County it is) this book by showing you the longitude and latitude, shows
you the long square where you may find it in the map: so that you need look no
further for it then that long square, or degree, and having found the town in
the map, it also shows you the County it is in.

If you be to travel to any place, for which you have no direction in this
book, first find the Town in the map that you are to go from, and also the Town
you are to go to; then lay a thread straight upon the map, from the one Town to
the other, this thread does show you the Towns in the nearest way, if you could
travel as straight as you can look. But because the ways are generally crooked
therefore you are many times enforced to go out of the streightest way, but be
sure you go not far from it, for the nearer you go to that straight line, the
less you go about.

If you be to travel to any Town not named in the map, then you must learn
between what two Towns named in the map the place you desire to go to stands:
and (according to the distance from either of them) suppose it were placed in
this map; then find your way as aforesaid, by laying your thread to that
supposed place; and thus you may do if you be to travel to a place not named in
the map. Thus much for the map and the use thereof

Of the Table of the Highways.
Forasmuch as many Country people do not understand what a Collumn is, which
they must do before they understand the directions for the highways: I shall
therefore explain the meaning thereof by an example or two; that is to say:

Look in the 41 page, you will find the second Town thus: 1. Dulverton 16. 131.
now in this example the place where 1. stands, is called the first Collumn, the
place where Dulverton stands is the second Collumn, the place where 16. stands
is called the third Collumn, and the place where 131 stands is called the
fourth Collumn.

In the same page look till you find 1 Chipnam, 10. 77. in this example 1. is
in the first Collumn, Chipnam in the second, 10. in the third, and 77. in the
fourth Collumn: and so of the rest. And now a word or two for the Almanac.

In the use of an ordinary Almanac, these things must be known; that is to
say, the year of our Lord, the month of the year, and the week of the month,
else a month or week may be mistaken. Now these being known, these tables are
as easy as any Almanac, after you know the Sunday Letter for the year
desired: which you may easily do by the table in folio 59, and 60. Example;

Suppose the year 1655. in the first Collumn you have the year 1655. in the
second Collumn you have 3 which is the Golden Number that year, in the third
Collumn you have g. which is the Sunday Letter that year, in the fourth Collumn
you have 3. which is the Epact, in the fifth collumn you have 25. which is the
number of direction for that year, by which number of direction, and by the
Table of the moveable feasts, the said feasts may be known for that or any
other year, till 1701.

A KEY For the Opening or Explaining of the MAPS.
BEfore we come to the use of the Tables, the Reader is desired to take notice
that the top, the bottom, and the sides of this Map are divided into certain
black and white parts; ten of which parts on the top, or on the bottom make a
degree in longitude, and six of the parts on either side make a degree in
latitude; which degrees are figured as well on the top as on the bottom, to
show the longitude, and they are figured on either side, to show the latitude.

And you may please to note, that every of the black and white parts, or
divisions on the top and on the bottom, contain ten minutes; and each of the
white and black parts, or divisions on the sides, contain six minutes, and
sixty minutes make a Degree. From the beginning of every degree on the bottom,
to the beginning of the same degree on the top, are lines drawn through the
Map; these lines are called the lines of longitude. From every degree on the
left side to the same degree on the right side, there are also Lines drawn
through the Map, which are called the lines of Lat^^^^^e, as was said before.
This being known, the use follows.

How to find out readily any Town named in the Table, with the Longitude and
Latitude thereof.
Look in the Table following, proper to that Nation or Country which the
Town is in, and right against it you have the Longitude of the place in the
first Collumne, and in the second Collumne you have the Latitude of the same
place.

Example. Suppose that you would find the longitude and latitude of Bedford.
Look in the Alphabeticall table for Bedford among the Towns in England,
beginning with B. and right against it you have 20 in the first Column, which
shows that Bedford is in the 20 degree of Longitude: and in the second Collumn
you will find 52. which shows that Bedford is in the 52. degree of Latitude.

Example. Suppose that you would find Dublin in Ireland, then you must look
into the Alphabeticall Table of or for Ireland, amongst the Towns beginning
with the Letter D. and right against Dublin in the first Collumne you will
find 13. which shows that Dublin is in the 13. degree of longitude; and in
the second Collumn you have 53 which shows that Dublin is in the 53. degree of
latitude; and so of the rest.

How to find out readily in the Map, any Town named in the same.
HAving found out by the Rules aforegoing, the longitude and latitude of the
place, observe where the line of Longitude cuts the line of Latitude, and in
the long square above the line of Latitude toward the right hand, you may
find the Town desired.

Example. Suppose Cambridge be desired to be found, in the Map, I find that
the line of its Longitude is 20. and cuts the line of its Latitude which is 52.
in the which square near the line of Longitude 21. you may find Cambridge.

Suppose you wou^d find Chichester in the Map, you will find that the line of
its Longitude is 19. and cuts the line of its Latitude, which is 50. in a place
of the Sea below the Isle of Wight, marked with a little at above which, before
you come at the 51. line of latitude, and toward the right hand, before you
come at the 20 line of Longitude, you will find Chichester, the place desired.

Note that when a Town happens to be in a line, it must be accounted for that
degree which is figured at that line, and thus you may easily and readily find
out any place named in the Map.

Note that where you find two or more Towns of one name, you have the first
Letters of the County that each Town is in.

A Table of the Principal Roades in England and Wales, divided into three
Parts.
In the first Part, take notice, that there is belonging to each Road four
Collumes.
1 IN the first Collume against so many of the Towns written in a greater
Letter as are needful, is set one of the 24 Letters in order as they be in the
Alphabet, and in the same Collume against each Town written in a smaller
Letter, there is some one number, as 1. 2. 3. etc. which by the directions of
the second Part, readily shows you how to find any Town written in the
smaller Letter in this Part.

2 In the second Collume is set down the names of the chief Towns in each Road
in order as the said Towns lie from London, beginning with the furthest Town
in any Road from London, which furthest Towns are written in a bigger Letter,
and are set Alphabetically for the more ready finding out of any of them.

Example.
Suppose you would know the way from Dover to London, look for Dover in this
second Collume, amongst the Towns written in a bigger Letter (which as is said
before, are set Alphabetically) and under it in the same Collume, is set
Canterbury, Sittinborn, Rochester, Graves end, and Dartford, which shows that
you must pass through those Towns as you go to London, and so of the rest.
Note that sometimes a Road breaks off many miles before you come at London, in
which case you ever have a Letter and a Number which directs you the rest of
the way, as you will understand by the Second Part.

3 In the third Collume, against every Town, in every Road, is set a number
which shows the distance of miles betwixt it and the Town before it. Example:
In the said Road from Dover to London, against Canterbury, in this third
Collume, is 12. which shows that the distance between Dover and Canterbury is
12. miles.

4 In the fourth Collume right against each Town is set the distance thereof
from London. Example, against Dover in this fourth Collume is set 55. and
against Canterbury is set 43. which shows that Dover is 55. miles. and
Canterbury is 43. miles from London.

The Use of the Second Part.
IN this Part is set down Alphabetically all the Towns mentioned in the
former Part not therein set down Alphabetically, but Printed in a smaller
Letter, and to each Town in this Part, there is set a Letter and a Number,
which Letter and Number if you find in the first Collume of the First Part.
you may therein also find the Town desired. One Example will make all clear.

Suppose you desire to know the way from Exeter to London: First look for
Exeter in the First Part amongst the Towns Printed in a bigger Letter, and
because yon find it not amongst them (which if you could do you need not to
look in this Part) therefore look in this Part for Exeter, and you find Exeter
in 2 now look for the same Letter and Number, viz. for M. in the first Collume
of the first part, and under that M. in the same Collume, look for 2. against
which 2. in the second Collume of the first part you find Exeter, and the
Towns in the way from thence to London, viz. Honington, Chard, etc.

Example.
Suppose you would know the way from Lincoln to London: Look in this part for
Lincoln, and you will find Lincoln l. 3. which directs you to look for L. in
the first Collume of the first part, and for 3. under it in the same Collume,
and against that 3. you shall find in the second Collume Lincoln, and under it
Sleford Bourne d. 2. which d. 2. directs you to look for the rest of the Towns
that you are to pass through, that is, Look for d. in the first Collume of the
first part, and under it look for 2. and against 2. you find Bourne, and under
Bourne you have West Deeping, Gunworth-ferry, Stilton b 17. which b 17. directs
you to look for B in the first Collume of the First Part, and under it is 17.
and against that 17. in the second Collume, is Stilton, Huntington, and the
rest of the Towns to London. This Town is put for an example, because if you
understand it, you will more easily understand other places which have fewer
references.

You may note that we go according to vulgar or common account, but in the
North and West they are often long.

The Directions. Having by the former Table found the Sunday Letter,
wheresoever you find that Letter in any part of this Table, it shows that day
to be Sunday: and right against it toward the left hand is the day of the
Month English Account: and on the right hand of it is the day of the Month
foreign Account.

Example, Suppose you would know what day of the week the 19. of Jan. 1651. By
the former Table I find the Sunday Letter was e: now because the Letter e in
this Table stands against the 19. day, I conclude that the 19. day was Sunday.

Example. Suppose I would know what day of the week the first day of March
1653. was: By the former Table I find the Sunday letter to be b, now I look in
the month of March against the first day, and I find d: now if b be Sunday,
then c is Monday, and d is Tuesday, which shows that the first of March is
Tuesday, and so of any other day or year. Note that on the right hand of any
Letter in this Table is set the day of the month Foreign Account.

Example. On the right hand of the letter g, against the 15 day of July is 25.
which day is the 25. day of July according to Foreign Account. Note that there
is 10. days difference between our Account and the Foreign Account, as you
wll see if you observe your former directions.

How to find out the age of the Moon.
TO do this it is necessary to know 2 numbers.

1. The number of every mon^h of the year, as March is the first month the
number thereof is 1. April is the second month, the number whereof is 2. etc.
January is the eleventh month, the number thereof is 11. February is the
twelfth month, the number thereof is 12.

2. The Epact for the year desired, which may be known by the former Table for
any year mentioned therein. Note that the Epact does not begin til March, so
that the Epact for Jan. and Feb. must be the same with the year aforegoing.

These two numbers being known, add them together, and substract the sum from
30. the remainder is the day of the Change that month.

Example.
Suppose I would know what time the Moon changed in July 1654. the number of
the month is 4. the Epact this year is 22. add 22 and 4 it makes 26. take 26.
from 30. there remains 4. which shows that the Moon changed the 4 day of July.

If the two numbers make above 30. look how much it is above 30. and take so
much from 30. and then the remainder is the day of the change.

If the month have but 30 days in it, you must take the remainder from 29.
