Explication of this Table.
1 The first column on the left hand, and last column on the right hand,
descending downwards, is years complete, from 1 to 21 thrice repeated. 2 All
the other columns betwixt them, concluded in three spaces or distinctions,
contain sums of money, whereof that which is in the top alone, by it self
is the principal sum first lent, which in the first space arises from 1 l.
to 9 l. in the second space from 10 l. to 90 l. in the third space from 100 l.
to 900 l. All the other columns have sums of money containing not only the
principal, but the increase thereof, and the increase of the increase,
according to the principal sum in the head or top, and several years in
the first column on the left hand, or last column on the right hand: as for
example, j l. principal amounts by interest and interest upon interest in 7.
years, to j l. 18. s. 11. d. ob. q. and so of all the rest. 3 For any other
number or principal sum, not mentioned in this table, whereof you would know
the interest with the principal, etc. break it into two or more parts and
seek their increases severally, and add their total sums: for example, 14.
l. in 7. years. I break 14. l. into 10. l. and 4. l. and I find that 10. l.
in 7. years comes to 19. l. 9. s. 8. d. ob. 3. q. and 4. l. in 7. years
comes to 7. l. 15. s. 10. d. ob. farth. which added together, make 27. l. 5.
s. 7. d. ob. q. and so much 14. l. amounts unto in 7. years. By the same
order you may know the value of any sum greater then is in the table.
The use of this Table.
1 By this table you may know readily what the principal with the interest and
interest upon interest amounts unto yearly for 21. years. 2 Likewise you
may know the increase of annuities, adding the sums together of all the
years save one, and in stead of that one year take the principal* thus, j l.
annuitie in 6. years by addition of all the sums under it for 6. years,
comes to 8 l. 9. s. 8. d. ob. 3. q. to which add the annuitie j l. and it
amounts to 9. l. 9. s. 9. d. ob. farth. and so much j. l. annuitie amounts
unto in 7. years. 3 To conclude, by this table you may value all money
bargains, for lands, leases, annuities, pensions, rents, in present
possession, or in reuersion, and all other contracts by the gain or loss
thereof, after the rate often in the hundred.
