PHRASEOLOGIA PUERILIS, ANGLO-LATINA In usum tyrocinii Scholastici. OR,
Selected Latin and English Phrases, wherein the purity and propriety of both
Languages is expressed.

Very useful for young Latinists, to prevent barbarismes, and bald
Latine-making, and to initiate them in speaking and writing elegantly in both
Languages.

By I. Clarke B. D. and Master of the Free School in Lincoln.

LONDON, Imprinted by Felix Kyngston for Robert Mylbourne, and are to be sold
at the sign of the Unicorn near Fleet-bridge. 1638.

The Printer to the industrious Schoolmaster, Scholar, or Reader whosoever.
Good Reader,
BE pleased for thy better directions in the use of this Puerilis Phraseologia
to take notice,

1. That the English of each Latin phrase in the propriety thereof, is printed
together with the Latin, in the other column of the same page, that so thou
may see and show thy Scholars, the force of each expression: where sometimes
also thou meetest with two Englishes to one Latin phrase, and e contra.

2. That when thou have caused thy Scholars thoroughly to read over, and peruse
both the Latin and the English Translation (which they must know is not
verbal or grammatical) thou may cause them to fold down the column, or
lay a paper upon it, and by trying their memory, may see where and when they
miss in rendering and repeating the same, out of the English into the Latin,
and contra.

3. That I conceive it most profitable for the beginners, to learn all the
phrases without book, which done, let some one in a form take the book and
appose his fellows in the same form, and thou shall see how they will with
great emulation and delight ambitiously strive to answer.

4. That it will quite for cost, to cause them to construe and parse each
phrase verbatim, for so they will soon perceive the absurdity of their own
base and bald translating verbatim, and will take pains to look out for
equipollent Latin expressions for their English, and contra.

5. That thou may take here and there the English phrase, and compose some
short continued speech or interloquutory Dialogue, and make them strive to
render it, in the idiome or propriety of the Latin phrase, which thou shall
soon find exceedingly to benefit them in speaking and understanding the
peculiarity of phrase in the Latin Tongue.

And for some further light herein, (if this suffice not) there be contrived
several Dialogues in the third Edition of my Dux Grammaticus, whereby thou
may be fully instructed concerning the use of these elegant Phrases or
fragments of speech, in making of Latins, wherein I make no question but thou
shall soon reap singular benefit, and by a nearer cut and more expedition,
discover to thy Scholars all the elegancies of Grammar and Oratory, and beat
them off from all barbarous and bald Anglicismes, or Latinismes, in speaking or
writing; which how much it may conduce to the common good in the training up of
Scholars in this way, I leave to the judicious to imagine: and to almighty God
who does please abundantly to bless his painful servants, and crown their
labors with happy success.
