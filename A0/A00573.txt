To take pains.
Art thou desirous of profit, praise, pleasure, or knowledge? take pains,
watch, study, leave nothing unattempted. Pains by day and by night is not
only to be devoured, but to be digested by thee. No garland is given to the
sluggard. God sells all things to man for labor. The Husbandman toyleth, and
does his earnest endeavor, he works out huge and almost Herculean labors,
that after many months he may reap the fruits of his hard labor. The Sayler
with sails and oars makes to the Haven through many dangers of the Sea.
Artificers pass the nights without sleep, beat the anvile day and night, bend
all the sinews of their body, and forces of their wit, and move every stone,
that they may know their art perfectly. The Scholar, the continual Soldier of
wisdom, watches by C^eanthes Lamp, drinks in the smoke of candle light
studies, devotes his whole endeavor to studies; that at length he may furnish
his mind with all kind of knowledge. Certainly God (as the Poet says) has
set sweat before virtue; neither may any man enter into the inner Temple of
Honor, but by the transitory Temple of Virtue.
