WITS LABERYNTH, OR, THE EXERCISE OF Idleness.

CONTAINING AN ARTIFICIAL TEXTVRE OF TWO THOUSAND TWO HUNDRED AND SIXTY
Figures, so placed and disposed, as by the help of a brief Direction for that
purpose, you may tell which of them any man thinks.

AS ALSO BY THE SAME OBSERVATION, TO discover any name, or number, that shall
be imagined.

BESIDES AN AMPLE AND LARGE SVBIECT FOR THOSE THAT affect such ingenious
recreations, by the sharpness of their own conceits, to draw out many other
delightful varieties.

FIRST COMPOSED IN ITALIAN BY ANDREA GHISI, AND NOW Englished and augmented.

The Authors Epistle to the Prince of Mantoa Don Francesco Gonzaga.
IN all humane actions, the passage from one extreme to another was ever
difficult and dangerous, and therefore for recovery of weariness procured by
labor and exercise either of mind or body, which of necessity men must do,
was sport invented, which is no other thing but the Exercise of Idleness. For
that it being by his own nature pleasurable, it might ease by little and
little the teadiousnesse and burden of saciety and lassitude, and so gently
draw a man to rest and quiet. Now because labors be of diverse natures, and
diverse (also) be the natures of the persons that undergo and endure them, to
the end that every one might find recreation proportionable to his disposition,
there is granted unto sport and pastime, the liberty of variety. Variety,
wherein nature so much gloryeth, and man's mind, and every sense of his so much
delights: which, not satisfied with all that the world can afforde thereof,
do run with restless feet, or rather wearylesse wings, but can never
run over the huge field and the large confines of things therein contained.
With this variety I being enamoured, by dressing and digging the small
Garden-plotte of my weak wit, have drawn thereout the fruit of this my
Laberinth, which to your Highness I humbly dedicate.

Your Highness most devoted Servant, ANDREA GHISI.

A direction for the understanding of the order of this Book.
YOu are first to observe, that the whole Book comprehends One and twenty
leaves, which are severally marked on the top of the margent with the Letters
of A, B, C, according to the order of the Italian Alphabet. Every opening of
the Book presents you with Threescore Figures or Pictures, aptly distinguished
into four quarters, two upon each side, and fifteen figures in every quarter.
Now if you would know which of those figures a man thinks, thus you shall
perform it. Let the party imagine any figure he pleases amongst those of the
two first Pages of the book, and having thought it privately to himself, let
him tell you only in which of the four quarters it stands. Then look with
what letter the name of the first figure of that quarter begins, and turn
to the leaf in the alphabeticall order which is noted with that letter: as if
the figure he thought were in the first quarter, the first figure thereof is
Melancholy which begins with M, and you must turn to the leaf which is
marked with the letter M on the top of the margent. This done, bid him seek
out his figure in the four quarters of that leaf, and tell you in which of
them he finds it there; which known, you must again as before, take the
first letter of the first figures name of that quarter, and likewise turn to
the leaf that is noted with that letter. Then the third time ask him in which
quarter of that leaf his thought ^^gure stands, which being told, mark
what letter is the first of the first figures name of that quarter, and also in
what place in the Alphabet that letter stands, as whether it be the first,
second, third, or fourth in number: and so begin to account the figures of that
quarter, calling the first figure A, the second B, etc. till you come to your
letter which is the first of the quarter last mentioned, and when you come to
that letter, the Figure it falls upon in reckoning so the letters by the
figures, is the figure that was first imagined.

But by example the matter shall be more easily conceived. Suppose one thinks
a figure in the first quarter, by name the Gnat, look then for the first
letter of that quarter which is M, then turn to the leaf marked with M, and
bid him search out his figure he thought, and he will find it in the fourth
place of the third quarter. He having told you it is in that quarter, look
with what letter the name of the first figure of that quarter begins, and
you shall see it is O, then turn to the leaf O, and bid him tell you in what
quarter he finds it there, and he will tell you it is in the fourth quarter of
that leaf. Observe then what letter begins the first figures name of that
quarter, and you shall find it to be D; and likewise in numbering the letters
according to the order of the Alphabet, that D stands in the fourth place, as
A, B, C, D. Then begin at the 1. figure of that quarter, and say upon it A,
upon the 2. figure next following B, upon the 3. C, and upon the 4. D, and so
you shall find where the letter D lights, that is the figure of the Gnat,
which the party first thought; And so consequently of all the rest.

Now for the numbers annexed to the pictures of the 4. first quarters of the
Book, if you would know which of them any man thinks; let him first mark
and observe to himself the picture where his number stands, then bid him
seek out that picture in the leaf M, where he shall find it matched with some
other different number, which number let him take and carry in memory to the
leaf S, where he shall likewise find that number placed with some other
picture. That picture let him seek out in the leaf Z, and only tell you what
number he finds thereto adjoined, from which take 6. and the remainder shall be
the number that was first imagined.

Example.

Admit the number imagined were 40. which stands under the picture of Honor;
seek out that picture in the leaf M, and you shall see it marked with 52.
which number (turning to the leaf S) you shall find placed under the figure of
Fortitude, and that figure sought out in the lease Z gives you 46. from which
take 6. and so rests 40. your number desired.

IF you would know by a near conjecture what name any one thinks (whether of
himself, his friend, or his Mistress) so it be no strange or new divised name,
but the usual proper name of a man or woman: thus by help of the former rules
you shall perform it:

Let the party which has conceived such a name in his mind, choose some figure
(amongst those of the first quarters of the book) which begins with the
same letter that his supposed name does: and having thought such a figure, you
shall understand which it was by the direction of the first Rule. So have you
certain knowledge of the first letter of his imagined name. Which reserving to
your self (without telling him as yet what picture he thought) bid him reckon
with himself how many letters there are in the name he thought, and choose a
picture that has the same number annexed to it, which number you are
sufficiently instructed by the second Rule how to find out, and having found,
you then know both the first letter of the name he conceived, and likewise how
many letters it contains: upon which ground it is easy to give so near a
guess to the truth, as shall not once fail in many trials. And so by this
means you may discover, both what name, what number, and what picture is
thought all at one time. But note that when you are to find out any name
beginning with A, or W, though generally you use the letters of the Italian
names of the pictures, and not of the English, yet there you must take the
first letters of the English words Adamant, and Wrath, because there is none of
the pictures names in Italian begin with those letters. By example this Rule
will be cleared of all difficulty:

Suppose one thought his Mistresses name, and her name were Elizabeth, the
first quarter of the first leaf of this Book presents him with a picture,
whose Italian name begins with the same letter, namely Essercito the Army.
And following the order of the first Rule, the first letter of the 1. figures
name of that quarter being M, you are thereby directed to the leaf M, where he
finds the figure or picture of the Army in the fourth quarter, whose 1. figure
is the Quail, which sends you to the leaf Q, and there his figure standing in
the 2. quarter, and the second quarters 1. figure beginning with B, you
conclude thereupon that the figure he thought was that of the Army, and
consequently that his imagined name begins with E, answerable to the first
letter of the Italian word Essercito. This kept in memory, you proceed to
inquire what number of letters the name contains, which being 9. and that
number conceived in his thought befindes it under the picture of Fate. Then by
order of the second Rule, seeking out that figure in the leaf M, it is there
marked with 21. and from thence resoreting to the leaf S, the same number
shall be found with the picture of the Fencer, who in the leaf Z has only
15. from which abate 6. there remains 9. he-iust number that was thought,
equal to the letters of the name imagined. Thus knowing that the name
begins with E, and comprehends nine letters, for that there is no other
ordinary name consisting of the like number and beginning with the same letter,
calling to mind what has past in all this proceeding, you resolve at once that
the figure he thought was the Army, the number 9. and the name Elizabeth.

To tell a man how much money he has in his purse, both Pounds, Shillings,
and Pence, being within the compass of three Pounds, this Book shall
likewise effect it, in this manner. First, for as many shillings as he has in
his Purse or conceaueth in his mind, let him find out a number answerable
thereunto in the first leaf of the Book, and likewise observe what picture
that number is placed withal: which Picture, bid him tell in what quarter it
stands, and then proceed (according to the first rule) till you come to the
knowledge thereof: which keep to your self, and at the instant bid him add
the number of his Pence to the number of his shillings, and give you the sum
of both, whilste in the mean time turning carelessely back to the leaf A,
you shall there discover the number of his shillings, standing with the Picture
he thought: which number separate from the whole sum he gives you, and so
have you in several numbers the just sum of shillings and pence, which he
first imagined.

Example.

Admit the sum which the party has or thinks were 2. Pounds, 6.
shillings, and 8. Pence. The number of shillinges then is 46. which number
being placed with the figure of the Griffin, in the third quarter of the first
leaf, gives him occasion to think that picture: and having told you where
his figure stands, you proceed on according to the direction of the first
Rule, till you know the picture he thought was that of the Griffin. Then
bidding him put the number of shillings and pence together, and in the mean
while returning to the leaf A, you see there 46. the number of shillings which
he thought, under the Picture of the Griffin: and separating that from 54. (the
total which he gives you of shillinges and pence together) then have you the
accomplishment of this Rule in two numbers, namely 46 and 8. expressing 2.
Pounds, 6. Shillinges and 8. Pence.
