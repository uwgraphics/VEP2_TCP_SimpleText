PREFACE TO THE READER.
IT is the Custom of well-Reformed Cities in GERMANY and ITALY, (Courteous
Reader,) that every year the College of Medicine visit all Apothecarie
Shops, and examine their Drogs, Simple, and Composed; as destilled Waters,
Syropes, Electuaries, Pillons, etc. and what-soever is fuisted, corrupted,
falsified, or any-wayes sophisticate, they throw them out: and all dry Herbs,
reserved two years (although perchance fresh) they cause burn: and after there
be new and recent Simples gathered and bought, they over-see the composing of
such things as should stand over year. Thus the Shop being replenished of
new, they cause print the Table and Tax of all those Medicaments, according to
the plenty of the year, and as Foreign Simples are bought, cheaper, or
dearer, from INDIAN and EASTERN Traders: so they renew ordinarily the Taxt
every year as the Almanacke, to the intent their own Recepts be faythfullie
answered, the Diseased relieved, and the People eased: who by virtue of such
Taxes can often times serve themselves, not employing Doctors at all Occasions.
Whether or not such laudable Forms were necessary in our lawless Art here,
any Man may judge; especially, if his Ears were filled with the daily
Complaints of the People, which have been hither-to kept up in ignorance of
the sincerity and worth of such things; alleging, Whatsoever Doctors
ordaines some to sell, quid pro quo, or at least to weigh every man by the
Purse, per justuiam distributivam, non commutativam: and so are forced to cause
Merchands bring home things they know neither the composition, nor dose, only
for the price cause, which being taken without adwise, redoundeth to their own
hurt, and our dishonor that are of the Art; having insteade of a
Commonweal, a Common-evill therein, and the whole Faculty made in evil
odor to the people: not that they have ever lacked learned Professors, but
because hitherto in all the North part of this Kingdom, there has been no
well-ordered Apothecarie Shop: a thing so admirable, that it merits a part in
the Records of the Realm.

True it is, that for redressing these Enormities, I have brought Home, with
great Charges, fresh Materials, and an excellent Artificer, to make all things
contained in this Table: more faythfullie and delicatelie to any Man's Eye, and
as cheap as they can be had out of FRANCE, FLANDERS, or LONDON. And now
seeing we have replenished the Shop with Simples and Compositions for a year,
being often required for a Copy of our Taxe-Board; for eschewing such frequent
copying, we have thought expedient to cause Print all, for the use of the
Country People, to whom chiefly we consecrate our Labors, and expects
therefore thanks, nomine publico, and approbation of the wysest sort: (whom we
request to signify unto us, by Word or Writ, of their Contentment) which will
encourage us to proceed to greater things, and make Voyages our selves to
other Countries, for choosing, and Home-bringing in great, of the best Drogs
(not relying, as others, which never crossed the Seas, upon the Factors
recommendation) also to renew every year this Take, yea, they may expect of
this same AUTHOR a more profitable Work for the Country.

Other-wayes, if Spirits of Contradiction will admit no Reformation, but
living ever by evil example, would tyrannize more solito, and gainstand Rules
whereby they might be redacted in Order, suppressing malis Artibus, such
things vn-come to light (qui mal*e facit odit lucem, veritas non quaerit
Angulos, if by their procurements, or of whosoever that can maliciously, as
the Spies of CANAAN, bring up an evil report upon the good Land, we find
little dispatch of these VVares (whereof hitherto praised be GOD, we can not
complain) we must likewise fall back from this Bread, to A^khornes with
them, and (as they say) cast no more Pearls before Swine: thinking it
sufficient to show our good meaning to the Commonwealth, in erecting such
profitable Works, and let the Blame hence-foorth lie on others, not ASSISTERS,
but RESISTERS there-to: as being almost fatal unto this NATION, that
public Works ^eldome thryue; as for things already composed, we can
imploye them in our particular Practice: and if any loss be, we shall
sustain it Reipublicae causa.

And, seeing that we are more cunning, now-a-dayes, to be evil Pratlers of
others, than good Doers our selves, if any Picke-quarrell, in alieno opere
male ingeniosus, object, All things usual other-where, not to be
comprehended within this Tax, we confess to have retrinshed Superfluities,
and things out of use; and have withheld some rare Compositions, serving to
our particular Employment, which we cannot as yet make juris publici: but they
are as many here as the Country may bear; yea, I fear more, a greater
number to exclaim in the contrary, of too much variety, and more than our
Fathers used. But they shall know, that our Praedecessors for the most part, in
Ages past, were strong Men in personage, dwelling in Hilly Countries,
observing exactlie Hippocrates his Rule, Pa^ci in victu, eoque simplici and
impigri ad labores. Yea, somewhat in Policy barbarous, (absit invidia verbo:)
But this delicate declyning Age brings out frailer Bodies, and more diseases:
some advancing the same by immoderate Studies, exhausting their humidum
radicale and calidum innatum; and clambicating their best Spirits, so leaving
no remains, but the bitter Dregs of Humors, to make the rest of the life
anxious and unpleasant. But the number of such are few, compared to the rest,
that by commerce with other Nations, do not as Salomons Sayleres to Tarshish:
for they leave behind them the Gold of Ophyr; that is, Virtue and Policy,
wherewith they should plant their Country at their Return: and brings Home
only the Apes and Peacockes of those Merchandes; that is, Vanities, and
Foreign Vices, where-by they infect the Kingdom: Such is, Variety of Dishes,
Curiosity of Disertes, Sawses, Confections, Wine, and Strong Drinks,
Ydle-seat, and Venerie: perverting thereby all Digestions, corrupting the
Blood, and disturbing the harmony and proportion of Humors: and consequently
incurring worlds of new Infirmities, unknown to former Ages; which Evil,
since it is come to such a height now, that it is easier to be lamented, than
redressed, we think it not amiss, that Reamedies in exquisiteness and
variety march, pari passu, with Diseases.

But, to the purpose, for understanding of the following Table, it is requisite
to know, That Leaves of Herbs are measured by hand-fulles, and an hand-full
is noted thus (m.) Flowers, by halfe-hand-fulls, which are noted (half m.)
other dry Wares and Liquors are measured by weight, whereof the first is a
pound, answearing nearely in measure to our Scots Mutchken, and is marked so
(lib.) our pound contains twelve ounces: the ounce is thus noted (vnc.) and
contains eight dragmaes: a dragma is noted so (drag.) and contains three
scruples: a scruple is written so (scrup.) and contains twenty grains: a
grain is marked so (gra.) and is the least of our weights, answering to a
Pepper corn: and the more that is bought of any thing, it is the cheaper; and
the less, the dearer: as is usual in all Merchandise.

The Prices are redacted to the lowest of this year: and as they change
heere-after in other Kingdoms, whence we have many Simples, so this Tax may
be yeerelie reformed, as we have begun by this first: which, if it has not
preciselie hit on every price, at least will serve ever in time-comming, for a
groffe guess (as we speak) of the valor of such Wares: For Merchandise may
height somewhat, but not often unto the triple, and less to the quadruple,
quintuple, etc. This is said, for those that abusing the Peoples ignorance,
under pretense, that Practice is rare, and they must live by their Calling,
takes six prices for one thing: where-as, if Mediocritie were used,
Employments would be more frequent, and their Consciences better. And for more
certainty to the People, it were convenient that what Doctors ordain, were
given the Patientes by writ, and they to collation the Ingredients and
Compositions with the Tax: so should they not be deceived. If any curious
man of his own accord would buy any things of this Table, he shall be
answered; providing always he first inform himself, with us, of the use
thereof, (which advise shall cost him nothing:) and not applying them
impertinentlie, bring a slander on those he bought them from. And to that
effect, howbeit we make long Voyages, South or North, we shall leave behind
us a sufficient person in the Shop, to answer all buyers, and inform them of
the use of the things bought: and our selves shall not fa^le to be present (if
GOD please) at WHITSONDAY and MARTINMES COURTS, preciselie.

To those Simples which either Herballes, Gardiners, or Dictionaries could
furnish us, we have put to first the English, and then the Scottish names:
although barbarous, at least to make them known unto every man: To the
intent, that if any of those be cast by-handes, in Families, or rot
over-yeere in Gardens, send them to us, they shall be contented for the same.
As for Compositions, few of them have Vulgar Names, but the Title before
them: and the Table of the Simples declare where-of, and how, they are made.

If any of the People find our Medicamentes too dear, we are not
malecontented, that they go to others, (having not-with-standing first
essayed, if any Deduction may be made) providing they be eased, which is the
chief thing we aim at. If, on the other part, any of our Brethren will not
accord to our prices, come to us, they shall not be refused. Rex IVPITER
omnibus idem, Fata viam invenient.

And lastly, as for those that out of their ridiculous humor jested most at
our PROGRAME, whether at ALCHIMIE or REMEDIES, when they are convinced with
multitudes of Exemples, which they shall bear of (GOD willing) to their
confusion, ere it be long: Advise them, whether their Malice or Ignorance was
greater, to take exceptions at our Age and remaining place, and argument so
wittily, saying, Such things we never saw, nor knew: Ergo, they are
impossible, quasi ver^* as if there might not bigg Volumes be composed of
things which they never yet saw nor know, how lofty so ever their carriage
be. And thus we are forced, in stead of short PREFACES, to mix Apologeticall
Digressions: rather to prick some empty Bladders, puffed up with the VVynde of
Conceit, and to cloze the Mouths of those Braine-sicke Raylers, who suffer no
Action to escape their miss-constructing Commentaries; than other-wayes to
humor these our Domesticke Chamaeleons, who feed upon the Air of idle
Discourses. Which Prolixitie, notwithstanding, and Freedom in Arguing, hoping
thou will take in good part, I bid thee heartily Farewell.
